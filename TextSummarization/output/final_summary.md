# Summary

## Main points
- Child malnutrition (underweight/stunting) remains substantial globally and in Iran, with documented underweight and low body weight among Iranian schoolchildren.
- Iran’s National Free Food Program (NFFP) targets deprived elementary schools but has major gaps: limited coverage in big-city slums/poor urban areas, imperfect targeting (some recipients not poor), and snack packages that are nutritionally inadequate and lack variety.
- An interventional study in Shiraz, Iran (2009–2010) applied a public health advocacy process model to improve school snack quality and nutrition education, with evaluation of BMI-for-age and nutrition knowledge.
- Post-intervention, underweight (BMI-for-age < −2 SD) decreased significantly in girls in the non-welfare/poor group, but not in boys; overall change was not significant when not stratified by sex. Nutrition knowledge improved significantly.

## Important details
- Burden/context:
  - Global (2011, developing countries): 314 (296–331) million children <5 stunted; 258 (240–274) million underweight.
  - Iran examples: Sistan & Baluchestan high school girls—16.2% underweight; Tehran elementary children—malnutrition 6%–16%; Shiraz elementary students—~16% malnutrition/low body weight.
  - Recommended snack benchmark: ~300–400 kcal and 5–10 g protein/day.
- Study setting/design:
  - Shiraz, third urban region; 2009–2010; approved by Shiraz University of Medical Sciences.
  - Schools: 2 elementary + 1 middle school; students screened n=2,897 (ages ~7–13/14).
  - Grouping (convenience): poor/malnourished (“welfare/indigent”) n=561 (19.5%) vs well-off/well-nourished (“non-welfare”) n=2,336 (80.5%).
  - Measurements: weight (SECA, 0.1 kg), height (stadiometer, 0.1 cm); BMI and BMI-for-age z-scores calculated (CDC tools referenced) with WHO/NCHS standards and WHO z-score cutoffs also described (thinness < −2 SD; severe thinness < −3 SD; overweight > +1 SD; obesity > +2 SD).
  - Questionnaire reliability: Cronbach’s alpha = 0.61.
  - Analysis: paired t-tests (pre/post), z-tests for proportions; SPSS v17; p<0.05.
- Intervention (6 months) via advocacy process model:
  - Stakeholders: education organization/MOE, welfare organization, Deputy for Health (Shiraz University), Food & Cosmetic Product Supervisory Office, NGOs/charities; governors and Parent–Teacher Association involved for coordination/policy support.
  - Components: (1) education/justification campaign, (2) nutritional intervention (safe, diverse, traditional snacks), (3) networking.
  - Snack package replacement: average ~380 kcal and 15 g protein, with sufficient calcium and iron; poor/malnourished students received snacks free; others prepared snacks themselves.
  - Education: grade-specific booklets integrated into schooling; parent meetings.
  - Media/community mobilization: 15 TV/radio talk shows and 12 newspaper pieces on healthy diet, breakfast/snacks, unhealthy habits, and family role.
  - Funding: Shiraz University of Medical Sciences, charities, welfare organization, and Fars province education organization.
- Outcomes reported:
  - Underweight (BMI-for-age < −2 SD) decreased significantly after intervention among non-welfare girls (p<0.01); no significant decrease among boys; no significant change in total population without sex stratification.
  - Knowledge score improved from 12.5 ± 3.2 to 16.8 ± 4.3 (p<0.0001).
  - Socioeconomic association (as reported): severe thinness/thinness higher in non-welfare vs welfare (OR 3.5, CI 2.5–3.9, p<0.001); overweight/obesity higher in welfare vs non-welfare (OR 19.3, CI 2.5–3.9, p=0.04).
  - Among “impecunious” girls, malnutrition decreased from 44% to 39.4% (reported as significant), with increased normal BMI-for-age (−2 to +1 SD).

## Decisions or conclusions
- Public health advocacy was selected as the core strategy to improve NFFP implementation (coverage, targeting, and snack quality/variety), including reaching poor children in big cities.
- The combined approach (advocacy + nutrition education + improved snack package, free for poor students) was implemented as planned.
- The intervention was concluded to be effective in reducing underweight specifically among poor/non-welfare girls and in improving nutrition knowledge; authors recommend creating a platform to expand/adapt the model nationally with regional tailoring and further study across diverse socio-cultural settings.

## Risks or open questions
- Mixed/unclear growth reference usage (CDC calculation tools alongside WHO/NCHS standards and WHO cutoffs) may affect interpretability/comparability.
- Impact was uneven: significant improvement mainly in girls; no significant overall change without sex stratification, raising questions about effectiveness for boys and population-wide impact.
- Scalability/sustainability challenges: mass production of acceptable snacks across regions/tastes, engaging food industries while managing marketing concerns, and maintaining continuous inter-sector collaboration; expansion depends on persuading national policymakers with an evidence-based implementation plan.
- Evaluation limitations: inability to collect blood samples to assess adherence/biomarkers; intervention focused on school-time snacks with limited data on full-day dietary intake outside school.
